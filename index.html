Motorsports Waterproofing and Dry Gear — Protecting Your Equipment from the Elements

Whether you're crossing creeks in a UTV, racing in the rain, or riding through roost — the right waterproofing strategy keeps your gear, electronics, and equipment operational.

Water is the enemy of motorsports equipment. Not just in obvious situations like river crossings or monsoon-season trail rides — water damage happens from roost, condensation, morning dew, unexpected rain showers, and the general moisture environment of off-road riding. Every motorsports enthusiast has a story about a phone, GPS, radio, or piece of expensive gear destroyed by water that they thought their equipment could handle.

Effective motorsports waterproofing isn't about a single product — it's a layered strategy that protects gear, electronics, tools, and vehicles at multiple levels simultaneously. This guide breaks down the complete waterproofing toolkit for off-road and motorsports use, from DWR treatments for textile gear to IP-rated electronics enclosures to sealed storage systems for trail cargo.

The Four Layers of Motorsports Waterproofing

A comprehensive waterproofing strategy addresses four layers of equipment protection:

  1. Personal gear: Riding suits, gloves, boots, and helmets that keep moisture off your body
  2. Electronics: GPS units, phones, cameras, radios, and control modules mounted on vehicles
  3. Storage: Dry bags, waterproof cases, and sealed containers for valuables and gear
  4. Vehicle systems: Electrical connectors, wiring harnesses, and mechanicals that live in the splash zone

Most riders focus exclusively on personal gear waterproofing and ignore vehicle systems — until a failed electrical connection turns a great ride into a recovery operation.

Waterproofing Riding Gear — DWR Treatments

🏆 Best Foundation Treatment

DWR Spray-On Waterproofing for Textile Gear

Durable water repellent (DWR) finishes are the backbone of textile gear waterproofing. New gear typically ships with factory DWR applied — the water beads and rolls off. After 10 to 20 hours of use, DWR diminishes and water begins to soak in rather than bead. Reapplying DWR restores this protection fully, and the process is simple enough to do at home between rides.

Motorsports-specific DWR products from Nikwax, Grangers, and similar brands outperform generic fabric protectors because they're formulated for the abrasion, heat, and chemical exposure common in motorsports environments. Apply to clean, slightly damp fabric for best penetration and activation. Heat-activate (hair dryer or tumble dry low) after application for maximum bonding.

  • Restores factory-level water repellency to worn gear
  • Motorsports-specific formulas resist abrasion and heat better
  • Apply every 10 to 20 hours of heavy use or when water no longer beads
  • Works on gloves, suits, jacket liners, and boot uppers

Waterproof Dry Bags and Storage Systems

Roll-Top Dry Bags for Trail and UTV Use

Roll-top dry bags are the gold standard for protecting valuables and gear during off-road and trail riding. Unlike zip-close bags that can fail under pressure or after repeated use, roll-top closure systems create a watertight seal by rolling the bag opening at least three times and securing with a buckle. This closure method is rated for submersion — not just splash resistance — making it appropriate for water crossings.

For UTV use, 10 to 20 liter dry bags fit perfectly in the cargo compartment and behind the seat. Motorcycle riders typically use 5 to 10 liter bags that secure to the luggage rack or frame. Look for 500D or heavier PVC-coated nylon construction with welded (not stitched) seams — stitched seams allow water ingress even when the fabric itself is waterproof.

  • Roll-top closure: rated for submersion, not just splash
  • 500D PVC-coated nylon: durable and fully waterproof
  • Welded seams: no water ingress through stitch holes
  • 10–20L sizes work for UTV compartments and cargo beds

Electronics Protection — IP Ratings Explained

The IP (Ingress Protection) rating system tells you exactly how waterproof an electronic device is. Understanding these ratings is essential for motorsports electronics selection:

  • IP54: Protected against dust and low-pressure water spray. Not sufficient for UTV trail riding where roost and water crossings are common.
  • IP65: Fully dust-tight and protected against water jets. Minimum acceptable rating for UTV or motorcycle-mounted electronics.
  • IP67: Dust-tight and withstands immersion up to 1 meter for 30 minutes. Suitable for shallow water crossings.
  • IP68: Dust-tight and withstands continuous immersion beyond 1 meter. Best for electronics in high-water-crossing environments.

For any electronics you mount externally on a UTV or motorcycle — GPS units, light controllers, communication devices, action cameras — IP67 is the practical minimum. Many action cameras and purpose-built UTV electronics are rated IP67 or higher, which is a primary reason to choose purpose-built motorsports devices over consumer electronics with minimal water resistance.

Waterproof Hard Cases for Critical Electronics

For equipment that's too valuable to mount externally — laptops, cameras, medical supplies, spare parts — waterproof hard cases provide the highest level of protection available. Pelican-style cases with pressure equalization valves, foam inserts, and positive latch closures survive full submersion and physical impacts that would destroy soft bags.

For UTV-based racing and adventure riding, a medium-size waterproof hard case secured in the cargo bed handles electronics, first aid supplies, and sensitive gear in one organized, protected package. The positive latch system ensures the case doesn't open accidentally under trail vibration.

Vehicle Electrical System Waterproofing

The most overlooked aspect of motorsports waterproofing is protecting the vehicle's electrical systems themselves. Most off-road vehicles are designed to handle some water exposure, but aftermarket modifications — additional lighting, winches, audio systems, and accessories — often introduce water ingress points that the factory wiring never had to deal with.

Dielectric grease is the first defense for electrical connectors. Applied to any connector terminal before joining, dielectric grease displaces water, prevents corrosion, and ensures reliable electrical contact even after repeated submersion. It should be applied to every aftermarket electrical connector installed on a trail vehicle.

Self-amalgamating (self-fusing) tape provides waterproof sealing for wire penetrations and connector joints. Unlike standard electrical tape that unwraps over time, self-amalgamating tape fuses to itself to create a solid rubber coating that's impervious to water and vibration. It's the correct solution for waterproofing wiring harness splices and connector entries in off-road vehicles.

Frequently Asked Questions — Motorsports Waterproofing

What is the best way to waterproof motorsports gear?

A combination of DWR (durable water repellent) spray treatments for textile gear and waterproof storage bags for electronics and valuables provides comprehensive protection for most motorsports applications.

Are waterproof bags essential for trail riding?

Yes. Electronics, wallets, medications, and any paper-based items should be in rated waterproof bags on trail rides. Water crossings, roost, and unexpected rain can destroy unprotected items in minutes.

What IP rating should motorsports electronics have?

IP65 is the minimum for any electronics mounted on a UTV or motorcycle that will face trail riding conditions. IP67 and IP68 are recommended for devices likely to experience submersion during water crossings.

How do I protect my UTV's electrical system from water damage?

Use dielectric grease on all connector terminals, seal any aftermarket wire penetrations with self-amalgamating tape, and ensure all wiring harness connections are properly clipped and routed away from wheel roost paths.

Can you waterproof motorcycle boots and riding gear at home?

Yes. DWR-based spray treatments restore water repellency to worn textile and leather riding gear. Nikwax, Grangers, and similar brands offer motorsports-specific DWR products that work better than generic fabric protectors.

What dry bags are best for UTV storage?

Roll-top dry bags in 10 to 20 liter capacity work well for UTV storage compartments and cargo beds. Look for 500D or 600D PVC-coated nylon construction with welded seams for true waterproofing rather than water resistance.

How do I protect a GPS or phone mounted on a UTV?

Use a waterproof phone mount with a sealed cover rated IP65 or higher. Alternatively, mount the device inside a waterproof case with a clear front panel. External RAM mounts with sealed cases work well for trail navigation.

What causes most water damage to motorsports equipment?

The primary causes of water damage in motorsports are water crossings that exceed equipment ratings, roost from other vehicles spraying water into unprotected electronics, and condensation inside sealed housings that weren't properly dried before sealing.
